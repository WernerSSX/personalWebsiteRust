It is the year 2026. LLMs have been around for the past 4 years or so, disrupting the tech industry and showing the plebians (like me) on how to centre a div correctly, with no mistakes.

Most of the internships that I went through, I relied a LOT on LLMs. In my experience, they were like optimal search engines able to provide a roughly correct answer that I only required a few tweaks to fix. But the constant LLM prompts made me realise that my foundation on tech stuff (esp. algorithms) were weak, so I decided to take the step to improve on them.

Coming into UC Davis for exchange on 2nd Jan, I joined the CP club 2 weeks later.

I am no stranger to LeetCode. I did plenty of them (like 100+) in Python, even before entering NTU CS. The problem is getting the knowledge to stick (as I did not memorize common questions). Time to do all that again (sigh).

Every class, we learnt about a completely new data structure (eg DSU, Kruskal), and had some practice problems with it.

After some time, I went for 2 separate Codeforces Div 3 contests and solved like 3 questions each in Rust (because it is the best programming language).

Anyways, here are some insights that I derived from these few weeks:

##################################################################################
Most Div 3 contest questions are all from a pool of these topics:
1. Arrays & Sort
2. Binary Search
3. Two pointers / Sliding Window
4. Greedy
5. Heap
6. DP
-> I am currently trying to master these questions by memoizing all solutions of the Neetcode 150. At the time of when this is written, I can do 40/60 of them in under 15 minutes. Probably a good idea to recite this like the national anthem.

Before even approaching a question, we should tune our cheatsheet.
-> I was writing in C++ before I decided to switch to Rust. And it does not have the luxury of cin >> input. This forced me to write macros for cin and cout. It is from here I realised that you can predefine plenty of common functions and reuse them easily (without having to type out the whole implementation every time). Free cheat code.

We should also tune our testcase to account for min, max and a number in between
-> This increases the likelihood that the first submission is immediately accepted as you do not assume that test input pass == correct.

Maybe link to the Codeforces API for faster submission.?
-> I may try this out next time

##################################################################################
